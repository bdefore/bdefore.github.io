Slow Days
August 04, 2003

Maybe it's the reintroduction of caffeine into my life, maybe it's because I have increasingly learned to be calm and patient in the name of tact, but I have noticed I have become quite opinionated about things that I can afford to be.

Why else would I feel the urge, nealy surrounded by a province-wide raging inferno, to take some man's literary work down to the beach and responsibly yet ritualistically light it afire? I'm not a pyro, honestly, but I do feel very disturbed to think that a bestseller such as this has lined the pockets of a parasitic single-celled amoeba disguised as an author. There is no appropriate fate for this tome than exorcism by fire.

Whoa, reign me in.

Stepping back, yes I am still in Kelowna, however, due to the tourist craziness of the town's busiest week of the year, I've retracted back into my shell to plot the details of my remaining two weeks here. I do enjoy this process, almost as much as the spontaneous period which cyclically follows it. I've got a drawing board here which is still very much in a state of flux, but certainly my favorite plot at the moment is figuring out how to convince a local restaraunt to let me paint the huge steel exhaust pipe on their roof into the head of Ogopogo. Arts and crafts time is coming. It's been too long.

In the meantime, I'm living life pretty slow out here, coiling if you will. I've had a lot of time to read, including texts by Arelene Gaal and John Kirk, the resident local experts. Both are pretty good reads. Then there's Bryson, who's In a Sunburned Country (about Australia) exceeded my expectations and has me thoroughly excited to go Down Under. Before I fall off the edge of my precipic here, let me reassert that Bryson's work is masterfully reseached and hilarious, if a bit whiny at times.

Now I can come full circle and mention a book called Me Talk Pretty One Day by David Sedaris. It's been recommended to me by a few people, so when I was offered a free copy I gladlly accepted and started digging into it. On the back, it proffers a story of an American's impression of learning the French language. Sounds like a worthy topic.

However, he does not even mention French until page 168, halfway through the book. I feel lied to. All right, so perhaps I'm not entirely sure of that since I stopped thoroughly reading around page 15 when it became painfully obvious that he was going to spend dozens of pages on what life is like as a 10 year old with a lisp. He then goes on to recount his life as a sock ball artist and his various addictions. For over a hundred pages this is nothing more than the airing out of a very rank closet, with the special narrative tone of an ADD-worshipping valley accent. It's amazing how authentically this reads like "reality television" sounds.

Once he actually gets to France, we're introduced to dizzying quotes like, "There are plenty of places on eath where visiting Americans are greeted with great enthusiasm. Unfortunately, these places tend to lack anything you'd really want to buy. And that, to me, is the only reason to leave home in the first place."

Later on, "As an American, you're bolstered by an innate sense of security. Something goes wrong, and you instinctively think, 'We'll just call the embassy and see what they have to say.' People know where America is on the map. They know that it's loud and poweful. With certain other countries there's no such guarantee. 'Oh, right, Laos," I once heard someone say to a dinner guest. 'Didn't we bomb you a couple of times?'" I wish it was there was a hint of sarcasm, but instead this is all confessional.

This is why this book begs to be burned on the beach. This is why David Sedaris should be stranded and forgotten somewhere in unforgiving outback.

And this is why I should not drink caffeine.
