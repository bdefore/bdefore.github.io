Some History of Nahuelito

As pointed out by Peter Costello in his book, In Search of Lake Monsters, the famous Nessie of Loch Ness was not the first lake-monster to gain international recognition. Indeed, such an honor is a dubious one, at best, to determine. However, Costello's theory revolves around a lake-monster with much less ubiquitous pomp.

The legend holds much of its origin to the informative descriptions of American gold prospector Martin Sheffield. He apparently witnessed, "an animal with a huge neck like a swan, and the movements made me suppose the beast to have a body like that of a crocodile." To this day, swan-like sightings continue in this remote corner of Argentina's Patagonia.

These words of an exotic-yet-beautiful beast, somewhere off in the distant reaches of the world, fed the curiosity and appetite of Europeans, who had, by the turn of the century, become fond of tourism. Back at the homefront, Sir Arthur Conan Doyle would pen The Lost World which would be serialized in the The Strand in 1912. Within was a story of a lake-monster, or, as Doyle would describe it, a "creature like a huge swan, with clumsy body and a high flexible neck." Costello proposes that such imagery would become part of the popular consciousness. Not long afterwards, in 1922, came the first theories that what lay within Lago Nahuel Huapi could be a plesiosaur. As Mark Chorvinksy notes, "The inevitable circular question of whether life is imitating art or art imitating life (or some permutation thereof) comes to the forefront in cases such as this."

Eleven years later, similar accounts would begin to circulate much closer to British Empire, in the Scottish Highlands.

Fossil discoveries of icthyosaurs have been found nearby Nahuel Huapi, furthering the mystery of the area. Now under the shadow of its cousin Nessie, Nahuelito still makes an appearance today. Also known as "El Cuero" (the leathery one) and "El Manto" (the cloaked one), sightings usually occur on clear summer days.
