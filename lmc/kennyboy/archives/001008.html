Idealism in Romania...the never ending battle

"I want to move."
"I want to go to America."
"I want to go to England."
"I want to go to Canada."
"I NEED TO LEAVE ROMANIA."

These are the words I hear everyday, and I hear them a lot. My students my colleagues! Romania is a beautiful country, and this generation has nearly zero hope left for it. In a meeting nearly two months ago in Suceava a British man was selling English textbooks. He reported a fact that I still remember, "Romania," he said, "was the only country in the world that had the 'Super Advanced' English text produced for it."
If not for Romania this textbook would not exist. To me, this is only proof at how great these people are at learning a foreign language.
Recently I have been assigning Kafka for reading practice after school. Other classes speak of their problems as teenagers, while others debate their new constitution - everything spoken in English. My students are both incredible and intelligent, but they all seem to want to leave.
Back in High School I learned the term "Brain Drain" to represent what it meant when an educated or intelligent group of people leaves their country and, in the end, hurting their mother country. I learned this term in association with Russia losing their scientists to foreign countries after the collapse of the Soviet Union. In Romania I witness some of the smartest people I meet here all making plans to leave - if they haven't already left that is.
This brings me to the title of this entry: Idealism...
Idealism, to me, has never meant someone who is naive or blind to what's going on around them - to some people I know that being ideal, naive and utopian are one in the same.
No, idealism to me is just having a positive outlook on life. Believing in the good of humanity, no matter what. Some of my professors and friends from college may call this either unrealistic or naive, but it is still me.
Today I encountered a strange form of idealism. One of my students, one of many that I've begun tutoring after school, told me that she was an optimist. She told me that she believed that Romania was, essentially, a good country that will eventually succeed both economically and politically. "However", she told me, "after I finish University I want to move to America." (On a short side note, to anyone that's interested, the American dream is alive and well in Romania, and I find this as a source of inspiration of how we should view our own country as the land of opportunity that it really is.) Overall, though, this is disheartening. All of these intelligent people want to leave. Even the one person I met that says Romania will eventually be a strong country.
Last summer one of my trainers advised us to try and convince our students to stay in Romania and "make it work". Fully acknowledging the problems that Romania is facing with "Brain Drain". I decided long ago that the decision to stay is not mine, and that I would do nothing to convince my students to remain here. I give them my honest up beat opinion on Romania's future in the world and in the European Union, but never once have I told them that I think they should stay.
I asked the student of mine who so adamantly wants to leave Romania; "why"? She would then turn to me asking how one person, like I claimed, could take control and change a country - the previous week we had been discussing Ghandi, Martin Luther King Jr., Vaclav Havel (I needed someone from E. Europe) and many other who had a significant effect in changing the world. I told her to start small. Volunteer. March into the Mayors office and ask what she can do. Ask others where she can begin to help build and assist others in taking control of their businesses and surroundings. I told her to start small and build up larger and larger. Create her own power I told her, and then take over from the inside. This may sound ideal in it's own way, but I think that in Romania it might prove to be more effective than letter writing campaigns and sit-ins.
At this point is where I hit the wall: "we can't change anything on our own."
OUCH...this was my enthusiastic class too...don't let me get started on my apathetic 12th graders!
At this point is when I unveiled my idealism.
"You can change the world if you can effect just one person,” I also admit to them that "I would not be in Romania if I did not believe I was changing the world." I truly believe this too. I honestly think that every time I talk to my students, and give them encouragement, there is some good that comes from it.

These last few weeks have been pretty difficult. Some students are not showing up and others aren’t doing any work or speaking. These classes, above, that I am writing about, are some of the smartest and most up beat. These students inspire some hope within me, but I’m struggling to find ways to return the favor.
