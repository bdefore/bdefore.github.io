Adventures in Cooking: Part 1

The Self-Actualization of my Deficiencies

Well I have currently been at site for going on my third day now, and have successfully cooked for myself all three of these days (not once have I set foot in a restaurant as of this being written). For those of you who know me really well out there, or at least those of you who have lived with me for any period of time, I should define what I mean by “successful”. For me, success in the area of cooking is defined in two ways:
1- Upon reaching what appears to be the end of current attempt at cooking I, Ken Bonneville, can place whatever substance I created in my mouth, eyes being open is not a necessity, and can consume said substance with a minimal amount of gagging.
2- In the past three days the pasta I have purchased has remained within its original packaging. Which to me marks a true sign or a real effort in the kitchen being given.
With all of that being said and done with I, Ken Bonneville, have been relatively successful in my early attempts at cooking. I’m not overly hungry, although I may try frying some potatoes after this meal (fried potatoes have still been my greatest accomplishment to date because I didn’t need to close my eyes to eat them), but I am very much satisfied.
I think I am learning some very important lessons from learning how to cook, and I don’t mean just learning how to prepare food for myself. First off, cooking is hell of a lot harder than I ever gave anyone credit for (and by this mom…I owe you so many apologies at the moment).
Second, and most importantly, is that spices and sauces can fix most anything. Keep in mind, I said sauces and spices can only fix most items you have just cooked. I’m learning this the hard way because no matter how much hot sauce or soy sauce that I keep adding to my “fried rice” at the moment it still doesn’t change the shitty oatmeal type texture sprinkled with onions and peppers that I’m thoroughly enjoying at the moment!
Third, dark beer with you meal can be a valuable source of both calories and creating an artificial feeling of being full post consumption.
Fourth, if you’re gonna cook, then cook a lot! This enables you to store some away in the fridge and reheat later in the week. So a lot of cooking now leads to less cooking later, trust me.
Finally, I’m learning not to give up with my cooking. It’s been hard so far, but a little secret I’ve discovered is that the sooner I give up the sooner I go broke and insane from eating pizza every night for the next two years (this would be easily remedied if a cheap and decent Mexican restaurant were to be installed). Another reason not to give up is that the sooner I do the sooner I waste away. People reading this back home might be laughing at this concept, but I’ve lost at least 15 pounds this summer and am getting to the point where I can’t afford to lose much more. If I give up on cooking I’m sure to waste away into nothingness over the course of the next two years (However, if this did occur I don’t think my brother would mind an incredible amount. He’d inherit my guitars, stereo, car and the life insurance policy I filled out, naming him the sole beneficiary of).
All in all this experience is not being nearly as detrimental to my health as I may be implying it is. I’m still alive at the moment, so things could definitely be a lot worse. I’m only partially joking on this issue. I mentioned that the shear lack of food might lead to a severe wasting away; however, on top of this, if the food doesn’t kill me own ignorance might …one night of forgetting to turn off the gas and the next morning I’m a goner. I can see CNN now: “Two more soldiers have been killed today in Iraq by unknown assailants; violence in Israel is again on the rise; and North Korean leader Kim Jung Il is currently giving the rest of the world the finger. Finally, on a side note a young Peace Corps volunteer recently blew-up in his apartment in Romania; the CIA does not expect terrorism to be the cause of this atrocity.”
